What is Phishing?


Phishing is a type of social engineering attack that uses email, social media, and phone communications to deceive targets into providing their sensitive information. Attackers can achieve this by posing as legitimate organisations or individuals to gain the trust of targets. Moreover, there are two main types of phishing attacks to look out for:

Deceptive Phishing

This is the most common type of phishing. Deceptive phishing can be indentified as spam emails being sent out to millions of email addresses, and can accurately mimic trusted individuals such as a co-worker, popular organisations such as Google, or financial institutions such as NatWest. Furthermore, attackers will use techniques to manipulate your emotions, such as creating a sense of urgency or fear to increase the chances of you interacting wih an email.

Spear Phishing

This type of phishing attack is similar to Deceptive Phishing, however instead of sending many emails to a large amount of random targets, this attack targets individuals. For example, if there is a high-ranking individual in an organisation, they are more likely to have access to sensitive information within that organisation. Therefore, they are more likely to be targeted. Attackers will research an individual and design an method of attack specifically for that individual, which can include the attacker posing as someone that the individual trusts.

What are the indicators of Phishing?

  • The sender's email address doesn't match an individual or organisation's email.
    You should always check the sender email for any differences.
  • The email subject or content creates a sense of urgency or fear.
    Such as: Critical Alert! Someone has logged into your account!
  • The email is requesting sensitive / personal information from you, this is very suspicious behaviour.
  • Grammar errors within the email's content.
    Especially when the email is sent by an organisation.
  • The email has suspicious links.
    You should always hover over links and check the destination before clicking on them. Furthermore, malicious links can be hidden behind legitimate URL shorteners such as tinyurl.com,
    therefore you should be careful.
  • The email has suspicious attachments.
    You should only open attachments from people you trust. Furthermore, common file types used in phishing to look out for are:
    .EXE, .PDF, .XLS, .DOC, and ZIP files.

How can you prevent Phishing?

There are many steps you can take to protect yourself from phishing attacks

  • Enhance your account security
    Ensure that your accounts have complex passwords to reduce the risk of an attacker guessing your password, and security mechanisms such as 2 Factor Authentication (2FA) to prevent attackers from gaining access to your accounts.
  • Ensure that your systems and software are up to date.
    This will prevent attackers from taking advantage of any security vunerabilites that outdated software may have.
  • Install security systems
    Installing security systems such as Anti-Virus and Firewall software will protect yourself from malicious websites and files that are linked to phishing attacks.
  • Stay Aware.
    Mostly importantly, stay constantly vigilant of the indicators of phishing, as attacks are getting more and more sophisticated every day.

What to do if you've been Phished?


If you believe you have been Phished, there are some main steps you can take to minimise the damage:

  • Disconnect any possible infected device from the internet.
    If a device has been infected as the result of a phishing attack, you must disconnected the device to prevent any malware from propagating across your network.
  • Change all of your account passwords
    Ensure that your passwords are changed to prevent anymore malicious activity from taking place.
  • Scan for any malicious files using your anti-virus software.
    To prevent an infection from doing more damage, run a scan to find any malicious files and remove them.
  • Contact your service providers
    If your device/account has been compromised, you must contact the provider for that account. Which could be your work's IT department, or your Bank.
